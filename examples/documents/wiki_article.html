Overview

Photosynthesis is a biological process used by most plants, algae, and some bacteria to convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. It is fundamental to life on Earth, producing the oxygen we breathe and forming the base of most food chains.

Photosynthesis is the process by which organisms convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen, using sunlight as an energy source.

Chemical Equation

The overall chemical equation for photosynthesis is:

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

This equation is a simplification. The actual process involves dozens of intermediate reactions.

Stages of Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis occurs in two main stages:

  1. Light-dependent reactions
  2. Light-independent reactions (Calvin cycle)

Light-Dependent Reactions

The light-dependent reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplast. These reactions capture light energy and convert it to chemical energy in the form of ATP and NADPH.

Water molecules are split during the light reactions, releasing oxygen as a byproduct.

Experimental evidence using isotope-labeled water (H₂¹⁸O) confirmed that the oxygen released during photosynthesis comes from water, not from carbon dioxide.

Calvin Cycle

The Calvin cycle takes place in the stroma of the chloroplast. It uses the ATP and NADPH produced by the light reactions to fix carbon dioxide into glucose.

  • Carbon fixation by RuBisCO
  • Reduction of 3-PGA to G3P
  • Regeneration of RuBP

Ecological Importance

Photosynthesis is arguably the most important biochemical process on Earth, responsible for producing virtually all atmospheric oxygen and forming the foundation of nearly every food web.
